A **sweepstakes casino** (also called a *social casino*) is an online platform where you
play casino-style games using virtual currencies under a promotional **sweepstakes**
model — not by wagering cash. Because there is always a free way to take part and the
play currency has no cash value, sweepstakes casinos are available to players in most
US states, including many where real-money online casinos are not.

This guide explains exactly how the model works, why it is treated differently from
gambling, where it's legal in 2026, how redemptions and taxes work, and how to play
responsibly. When you're ready to compare operators, see our

## The dual-currency model

Every sweepstakes casino runs on two separate virtual currencies:

- **Gold Coins (GC)** — a **play-only** currency with **no monetary value**. You use Gold
  Coins purely for entertainment. You can optionally buy more, but they can never be
  redeemed for anything.
- **Sweeps Coins (SC)** — the **promotional entry** currency. Sweeps Coins are obtained
  through promotions and a mandatory free method, and they can be **redeemed for prizes**
  under each operator's official rules once you meet the minimums.

The key point: you never *buy* Sweeps Coins directly. They come bundled as a free bonus
with Gold Coin purchases, through daily login rewards, social promotions, or a postal
mail-in request.

## What "no purchase necessary" means

The phrase **no purchase necessary** is the legal heart of the model. It's the same
principle behind a fast-food "collect and win" game or a soft-drink cap promotion: a
company can run a prize giveaway as long as there is always a free way to enter.

That free path is the **Alternative Method of Entry (AMOE)** — in practice usually:

- a **daily login bonus**,
- a **social-media giveaway**, or
- a **postal mail-in request** (the classic sweepstakes method, detailed in each
  operator's official rules).

Because a no-cost route always exists, buying Gold Coin packs is legally framed as an
*optional* purchase of entertainment value that happens to come with a *free* Sweeps
Coins bonus — not a bet. If a site ever removes the free path, treat it as a red flag.
Our reviews check for it.

## Are sweepstakes casinos legal?

**Sweepstakes casinos operate legally in most US states, but a growing number have
banned or restricted the model, so legality depends entirely on where you live.** Since

## Why this is different from real-money gambling

Real-money online gambling requires you to **wager cash to win cash**. The sweepstakes
model breaks both halves of that definition:

1. **No purchase is required** to participate, and
2. the play currency (Gold Coins) has **no cash value**.

That is the same legal framework that underpins decades of retail and mail-in sweepstakes
promotions. It's why these platforms operate in many states where real-money iGaming is
not authorized.

## How you get Sweeps Coins

There are four standard ways to accumulate Sweeps Coins, and none of them require you to
spend money:

1. **Free bonus with Gold Coin purchases.** When you buy a Gold Coin pack, the operator
   typically attaches free Sweeps Coins as a promotional bonus. You are paying for Gold
   Coins; the Sweeps Coins ride along at no cost.
2. **Daily and login rewards.** Most sites hand out free Sweeps Coins for logging in each
   day, sometimes escalating on a streak.
3. **Promotions and social media.** Follow-and-share campaigns, contests, and email
   offers frequently include Sweeps Coins.
4. **Free postal request (AMOE).** You can mail a written request following the
   operator's official rules to receive Sweeps Coins with no purchase — the entry method
   that keeps the whole model legal.

## Redeeming Sweeps Coins for prizes

Winning Sweeps Coins is only half the process — redeeming them has rules, and they
matter:

- **Playthrough requirement.** Bonus Sweeps Coins usually must be *played through* at
  least once (1× is common) before they become redeemable.
- **Minimum redemption threshold.** Operators set a floor for redemptions — commonly
  around 50–100 SC depending on the payout method and site.
- **Identity verification (KYC).** Before your first redemption you'll complete a
  Know Your Customer check — typically a government ID and proof of address. This is
  standard, legally required, and a good sign the operator is legitimate.
- **Payout methods and timing.** Redemptions are commonly paid via bank transfer,
  e-wallet, or gift cards depending on the operator; speed varies by brand.

## Do you pay taxes on redemptions?

**Generally yes — cash prizes from sweepstakes casinos are taxable income in the US, and
operators may issue a 1099 tax form for redemptions that reach reporting thresholds
(often $600 or more in a year).** You are responsible for reporting prize winnings
regardless of whether you receive a form.

This is general information, not tax advice. Because your situation depends on your total
income and state, keep records of your redemptions and consult a qualified tax
professional.
